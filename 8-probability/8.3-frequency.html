Relative Frequency — Does It Converge?
Set a true probability and flip the coin. Early on it's chaotic — but as n grows, the relative frequency settles toward the theoretical value. That's the whole point of probability: it's a long-run prediction, not a guarantee for 10 flips.

Cambridge tip: relative frequency = number of successes ÷ total trials. It's not the same as theoretical probability — it's an estimate based on experiment. The more trials you run, the closer relative frequency gets to the true probability. Cambridge questions often ask you to explain why more trials give a better estimate.
