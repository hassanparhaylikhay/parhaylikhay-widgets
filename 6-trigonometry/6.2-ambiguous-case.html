The Ambiguous Case — When SSA Gives 0, 1, or 2 Triangles
You know angle A, side b (from A to C), and side a (variable). Slide a up slowly — watch the triangle appear, split into two, then collapse back to one. This is the "ambiguous case" of the sine rule.
a = 60
0 … 200
A = 35°
15° … 75°
Cambridge tip: the ambiguous case only appears on harder A*-grade questions. When using the sine rule to find an angle, always check: is the second solution (180° − answer) also valid? If both give a positive remaining angle and valid triangle, state both. Mark scheme awards credit for finding both solutions.
